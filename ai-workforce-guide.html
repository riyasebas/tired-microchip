You've heard this phrase in all-hands meetings, demo hours, and Slack. Here's what it actually means.

Part 1: What Is an AI Agent, Really?

The simplest definition

An AI agent is software that can understand a goal, figure out the steps to achieve it, and execute those steps — making decisions along the way without someone telling it exactly what to do at each step.

That's the key difference from traditional automation. A workflow or a rule-based bot follows a predefined path: "If X, then Y. If Z, then W." Every branch has to be anticipated and built in advance. If something unexpected happens, the bot breaks or stops.

An AI agent reasons. It looks at the situation, considers the tools and information available to it, decides what to do, and does it. If the first approach doesn't work, it can try something else.

A practical example

Imagine an employee messages: "My laptop is running really slowly."

Traditional ChatbotAI Agent
  • Matches to a keyword or intent
  • Surfaces a knowledge article ("Here are tips to speed up your laptop")
  • Or creates a ticket and routes it to IT
  • Bot's job is done — a human takes over
  • Checks the employee's device data — CPU usage, memory, disk space, running processes
  • Cross-references with known issues for that laptop model
  • Finds a specific application consuming excessive memory with a known fix
  • Applies the fix directly — terminates the runaway process, clears the cache
  • Confirms with the employee that things are back to normal
  • If approval needed, explains what it wants to do and why, acts once confirmed
  • If too complex, escalates with full picture: device, what was tried, what failed, likely root cause

The agent didn't follow a script. It assessed, decided, and acted — keeping the human in the loop where it matters, and handing off with context when it needed to.

Where does Atom fit?

Here's something important: Atom is an agent. It has its own instruction file (more on this in Part 2), its own identity, its own skills. Customers can modify Atom's behavior, tone, and capabilities just like they would any other agent.

The difference is scope. Atom is the generalist — the front door that employees interact with. It handles the broad range of questions and requests that come in: "What's the Wi-Fi password?", "I need Salesforce access," "When's the next company holiday?"

Specialized agents are focused — each one covers a specific domain or function. A VPN troubleshooting agent. An onboarding agent. A ticket triage agent. An expense policy agent.

When an employee asks Atom something that falls within a specialist's domain, Atom hands off seamlessly — the employee doesn't need to know which agent is handling their request. They just talk to Atom, and the right specialist takes over behind the scenes.

Think of it like a team: Atom is the generalist on the front desk who can handle most things and knows exactly who to call in for the rest. Every team member — including Atom — is built the same way, with the same framework. Some just have broader scope than others.

Part 2: How an Agent Is Built in Atomicwork

The agent file: it's just a document

An AI agent in Atomicwork is defined by a structured document — think of it like an onboarding brief for a new team member. You describe who they are, how they should behave, what they're good at, and what tools they have access to. The agent reads this file and operates accordingly.

This is what we call the agent.md — a plain-English instruction file with four components.

A note on terminology: The exact naming and structure of these components may evolve as the product develops. What follows is a conceptual framework for how agents work — the building blocks are stable, even if the labels shift.
1 Identity ▶

What it is

The agent's job description — who it is, what it's here to do, who it serves, how it communicates, and what success looks like.

What you write

The agent's name, role, function, audience, purpose, tone, and communication style. This is where you define both what the agent is and what good looks like for it.

Example

Name: VPN Support Agent
Role: IT Support Specialist — Network Access
Audience: All employees who use the corporate VPN
Purpose: Resolve VPN connectivity issues without requiring human intervention. Success means the employee's VPN connection is restored, or — if the agent can't resolve it — a ticket is created with full diagnostic context and assigned to Network Ops within 5 minutes.
Tone: Professional but approachable. Avoid jargon unless the user is technical. Get to the answer quickly — offer detail only if asked.
When unsure: Say so clearly. Don't guess.
Reports to: IT Support Lead (human). Can also be invoked by the Coordinator agent (Atom) when a VPN-related request comes in.

Why it matters

Identity determines everything about how the agent presents itself and scopes its work. An agent with an IT networking identity won't try to answer HR questions — it'll hand those to the right agent. And an agent that triages P1 incidents for the NOC team should be direct and technical, while one that helps new hires with onboarding should be warm and patient. The purpose and success criteria keep the agent anchored — without them, it might go down rabbit holes or stop too early.

The "Reports to" property also establishes the agent's place in the organization. An agent can report to a human manager or to another AI agent — which means a manager agent can invoke and oversee its child agents, just like a team lead would delegate to their team.

2 Behavior ▶

What it is

The agent's general operating procedures — the rules, guardrails, and context it follows.

What you write

Guidelines, boundaries, defaults, and environmental context. When to escalate. When to ask for permission. What to never do. And importantly — what your organization's specific environment looks like, so the agent tailors its actions accordingly.

Example

Always verify the user's identity before making changes to their account.
If the issue involves data loss, escalate to a human immediately — do not attempt to resolve.
For password resets, confirm the user's email before initiating.
Never share credentials or security tokens in a conversation.
Our organization uses Zscaler as our VPN client — only reference Zscaler documentation and knowledge articles when troubleshooting VPN issues. Do not suggest solutions for other VPN clients.
When performing any action that modifies a user's device or account settings, explain what you're about to do and ask for permission before proceeding.

Why it matters

Behavior is the guardrails. It keeps the agent within bounds and ensures it operates the way your organization expects. This is where compliance and security instructions live, but it's also where you encode organizational context — like "we use Zscaler, not Cisco AnyConnect" — so the agent doesn't waste time on irrelevant solutions. A useful distinction to remember: behavior tells the agent how to operate and what context to operate within.

3 Skills ▶

What it is

Specific capabilities the agent has — the things it knows how to do.

What you write

Step-by-step instructions for handling specific types of requests or situations. Each skill is a focused set of actions.

Example — a "VPN Troubleshooting" skill

When a user reports VPN connectivity issues:
1. Check if there are any active incidents affecting VPN services
2. If there's an active incident: inform the user, provide the estimated resolution time, and offer to notify them when it's resolved
3. If no active incident: check the user's Zscaler client status and device compliance through Intune
4. If the issue matches a known resolution: execute the fix (e.g., restart the Zscaler service, renew the network certificate) — ask for user permission before making changes to their device
5. Confirm with the user that the issue is resolved
6. If unresolved after two attempts: create a ticket with full diagnostic context and assign to Network Ops

Notice the difference from a knowledge article. A knowledge article might say "If a user has VPN issues, ask them to check their Zscaler app and restart it." That's reference material — guidance for the user to follow. A skill says "Check the Zscaler app status, restart the service if needed, and confirm the fix." That's instructions for the agent to execute. Knowledge tells. Skills do.

How skills work technically

Each skill is a separate markdown file with its own instructions. At runtime, when a user's request matches a skill's domain, only that skill is loaded into the agent's context — not every skill the agent has. This keeps the agent focused and accurate.

Skills can be:

  • Inline — specific to one agent
  • Shared — reusable across multiple agents (e.g., a "look up user profile" skill that any agent can use)

Why it matters

Skills are the core of what makes an agent useful. Without skills, you have a chatbot with a personality. With skills, you have a specialist that knows how to handle your organization's specific processes — and acts on them directly.

4 Tools ▶

What it is

The external systems and actions the agent can access to get things done.

What you define

Which integrations the agent has access to and what it's allowed to do with them.

Examples of tools an agent might use

  • Okta — reset passwords, check MFA status, verify group membership
  • Jira — create tickets, update statuses, pull ticket details
  • Intune/JAMF — check device compliance, query hardware specs, trigger remote actions
  • Slack — send messages, post in channels, create threads
  • PagerDuty — check on-call schedules, trigger escalations
  • Workday — look up employee records, process leave requests
  • Any system with an API — agents aren't limited to pre-built integrations

How tools work

Tools are connected through MCP (Model Context Protocol) and our Call API capability, which give the agent access to any connected integration's full capabilities — not just a pre-built list of actions. The agent decides which tool to call, what inputs to provide, and in what order, based on the situation. We're also building out a coding agent that can generate code to execute workflows across systems on the fly — meaning if a connection or action doesn't exist yet, it can be created dynamically.

Why it matters

Tools are what turn understanding into action. An agent that can reason about a problem and directly fix it is fundamentally more valuable than one that can only describe the solution.

Part 3: What Makes Atomicwork's Agents Different

"AI agents" is a term that Salesforce, Microsoft, ServiceNow, and dozens of startups are all using. So why should anyone care about Atomicwork's version?

1
Universal Context: agents that already know the situation

Most AI agent solutions require the agent to go gather context before it can act. It calls one API to get the user profile, another to check their device, another to look up permissions. Each call takes time, and the agent has to figure out which calls to make.

Atomicwork's agents start with Universal Context — the combined picture of who the user is, what devices they use, what software they have, what permissions they hold, what their history looks like, and what's happening in their environment right now. This context is already there before the agent takes its first action.

When an employee says "my laptop is slow," an Atomicwork agent already knows:

  • What laptop they have (model, specs, age)
  • What software is installed and what's running
  • Whether the device is compliant with company policies
  • What issues they've reported before and how those were resolved
  • What team they're on and what permissions they have

This isn't a technical detail — it's the difference between an agent that resolves an issue in one interaction and one that spends the first three messages gathering basic information.

2
Built on real service management constructs

Most agent platforms start with "connect your tools and define workflows." Atomicwork's agents start with something those platforms don't have: a live, structured understanding of your service environment.

Agents in Atomicwork have native access to:

  • Service catalog — every service, request type, and catalog item your organization offers
  • Knowledge base — published articles, verified answers, ingested topics
  • Incidents and requests — open, in-progress, resolved, with full history
  • Assets — devices, software, licenses, and their relationships
  • People directory — employees, their roles, their teams, their permissions — and now, their agents
  • Workflows and automations — existing automation rules and escalation paths

This isn't a list of integrations. These are first-party constructs that the agent understands natively. When a triaging agent looks at an SLA-breaching ticket, it doesn't need to call an external API to understand what the ticket is, who it's assigned to, what the SLA policy is, or what the escalation path should be. It already knows — because that's all part of the platform it lives on.

3
Custom objects: agents that extend the platform

Most agents can read data and write data. Atomicwork's agents can define data.

Custom objects are data types that agents can work with — or create on the fly when they encounter a use case where the right data structure doesn't exist. For example, an agent could create a "Vendor" object with specific fields (name, contract renewal date, primary contact), populate it with entries, link it to related objects like "Vendor License" or "Vendor SLA," and make it available as a real entity in the platform with filters, views, and all the standard capabilities.

Why this matters: if an agent encounters a workflow where the data structure it needs doesn't exist yet, it doesn't have to wait for a product team to build it. It creates the structure, uses it, and moves on. Agents adapt to your organization's needs without custom development.

4
Connected to anything

Beyond the platform's native constructs, agents connect to any external system through MCP (Model Context Protocol) and Call API — giving them unrestricted access to the full capabilities of any connected integration, not just a curated list of pre-built actions.

We're also building a coding agent that can dynamically generate the code to execute complex, multi-system workflows. So if an agent needs to coordinate an action across Workday, Jira, and Slack — and no pre-built integration exists for that exact combination — it can create the workflow on the fly.

The result: agents aren't constrained by what integrations we've pre-built. If a system has an API, an agent can use it.

The bottom line: Other platforms give you AI that can talk to your tools. Atomicwork gives you AI that lives inside your service management environment — with real-time context about your people and devices, native access to your catalog, assets, and knowledge, the ability to extend the platform by creating new data structures, and unrestricted connectivity to any system. The agents don't just integrate with your operations — they operate within them.

Part 4: What This Looks Like in Practice

Agent 1: IT Ticket Triage Agent

What it does: Acts as an intelligent triage layer for a support team — reviews open tickets, identifies priorities, drafts responses, and recommends next actions.

How it was built ▶
IdentitySupport triage specialist. Purpose: ensure no ticket breaches SLA without action, and reduce time support leads spend on queue management. Direct, concise tone — this agent talks to IT leads, not end users.
BehaviorPrioritize by severity and SLA proximity. When drafting a reply, always reference the ticket history and relevant knowledge articles. Never close a ticket without confirmation from the assigned agent.
SkillsTicket queue review (pull SLA-breaching and stale tickets), response drafting (compose contextual replies based on ticket history), workload recommendation (generate prioritized daily task list for support agents)
ToolsAtomicwork ticketing system, Slack (for posting recommendations and summaries)
In practice ▶

A support lead starts their day by asking Atom: "What should the team focus on today?" Atom routes this to the triage agent.

The agent reviews every open ticket. It identifies five that are approaching SLA breach, three that have been waiting on a response for over 24 hours, and two that look related to each other (both reporting the same application error).

For the stale tickets, it drafts contextual replies — not generic "we're looking into this" messages, but responses that reference the specific issue, what's been tried, and what the next step is. The support lead reviews and sends them.

For the SLA-approaching tickets, it generates a prioritized list factoring in severity, complexity, and whether related tickets can be batched together. It posts this in the team's Slack channel before the morning standup.

For the two related tickets, it flags them as potentially linked to the same root cause and suggests they be investigated together.

The outcome ▶

The work that used to consume the first 45 minutes of a support lead's day — scanning the queue, mentally prioritizing, drafting responses to stale tickets — is done before they open their laptop. The team focuses on solving problems, not managing a queue.

Agent 2: Employee Onboarding Agent (HR Operations)

What it does: Handles the operational side of new hire onboarding — provisioning access, answering first-day questions, ensuring nothing falls through the cracks.

How it was built ▶
IdentityHR Onboarding Specialist. Purpose: ensure every new hire has everything they need — access, equipment, information — by end of day one, and surface any gaps to HR immediately. Warm, patient tone — this agent talks to people on their first day who are likely overwhelmed.
BehaviorAlways introduce yourself and explain what you can help with. For access provisioning, verify with HR records before granting. If a request falls outside standard onboarding (e.g., special software not in the standard package), escalate to the hiring manager for approval. Never provision admin-level access without explicit HR approval. Our organization uses Okta for identity, Jira for IT tickets, and BambooHR for HR records.
SkillsAccess provisioning (grant standard application access based on role and department), first-day Q&A (answer common questions about office logistics, benefits enrollment, org structure), onboarding checklist tracking (monitor which provisioning tasks are complete, follow up on pending items)
ToolsOkta (provision/deprovision access), BambooHR (pull employee records, department, role), Jira (create IT tickets for hardware issues), Slack (communicate with new hire and notify HR of gaps)
In practice ▶

Three days before a new hire's start date, the agent checks BambooHR for the employee's record — name, role, department, office location, hiring manager. It cross-references this with the standard access list for that role (e.g., all marketing team members get Figma, HubSpot, Google Workspace, and Slack).

On day one, it provisions the standard access through Okta, confirms each one succeeded, and messages the new hire on Slack:

"Hi! I'm your onboarding assistant. I've set up your access to Figma, HubSpot, Google Workspace, and Slack. Here are a few things that might be helpful today: [link to office map], [link to benefits enrollment guide], [link to your team's Slack channel]. Let me know if you need anything — I'm here all day."

The new hire asks: "Do I get access to Salesforce too?" The agent checks — Salesforce isn't in the standard marketing access list. It messages the hiring manager: "Your new hire has requested Salesforce access. This isn't part of the standard marketing access list. Would you like me to provision it?" The manager approves, and the agent handles it.

At end of day, the agent reviews the onboarding checklist. Everything is done except the hardware setup — the laptop hasn't been logged as delivered. It creates a Jira ticket for the IT team and notifies HR: "New hire onboarding for [name] is complete except for hardware delivery. I've flagged this with IT."

The outcome ▶

The HR team didn't manually provision a single application, answer a single "where do I find X" question, or chase down a missing laptop. The agent handled the operational checklist end to end, escalated where needed, and kept everyone informed. HR focused on the human side of welcoming a new team member.

Agent 3: Expense Policy Agent (Finance Operations)

What it does: Handles employee questions about expense policies and guides them through the submission process — reducing the volume of "can I expense this?" questions that finance teams deal with daily.

How it was built ▶
IdentityFinance Operations Specialist — Expense Policies. Purpose: help employees understand what's reimbursable and submit correctly, reducing rejected submissions and repeat questions to the finance team. Professional, precise tone.
BehaviorAlways reference the current expense policy document — do not improvise policy interpretations. If a request falls into a gray area, say so clearly and route to the employee's manager for a judgment call. Our organization uses Brex for corporate cards and Navan for travel bookings. For travel-related expenses, always check Navan first to see if the booking was made through the approved channel.
SkillsPolicy lookup (answer "can I expense this?" questions against the current policy), submission guidance (walk employees through the correct expense submission process), receipt validation (check if a submitted receipt matches the claimed amount and category)
ToolsBrex (check card transactions, match receipts), Navan (verify travel bookings), Atomicwork knowledge base (pull current expense policy), Slack (communicate with employee, escalate to manager)
In practice ▶

An employee messages Atom: "I took a client out to dinner last night — can I expense it?" Atom routes this to the expense policy agent.

The agent checks the current expense policy and finds that client entertainment is reimbursable up to $150 per person with a receipt and a brief business justification. It responds: "Yes, client dinners are reimbursable up to $150 per person. I'll need the receipt and a one-line note on the business purpose. Want me to start the submission for you?"

The employee shares the receipt — $320 for two people. The agent validates: that's $160 per person, which exceeds the policy limit. It tells the employee: "This comes to $160 per person, which is $10 over the per-person limit. I can submit it for the approved $150 per person ($300 total), or if you'd like to claim the full amount, I'll route it to your manager for an exception approval. What would you prefer?"

The employee asks for the exception. The agent messages the manager with the details, gets approval, and submits the full amount through Brex.

The outcome ▶

The finance team didn't field the question, look up the policy, validate the receipt, or chase down a manager for approval. The agent handled it end to end — accurately, within policy, and with proper approvals. The employee got their answer in minutes instead of emailing finance and waiting a day.

Part 5: Where This Is All Headed

⚠ Important: Everything in Parts 1–4 describes what's being built right now. This section is about where the pieces fit together. Do not make commitments to customers about timelines for anything below.

⏰ Proactive agents

The agents above are invoked — someone asks a question, or an alert fires, and the agent responds. The next step is agents that operate on their own schedule.

A triage agent that doesn't wait for someone to ask "what should I work on today?" — it runs every morning at 8 AM, reviews the queue, drafts responses to stale tickets, flags SLA risks, and posts a summary in Slack before standup. An onboarding agent that wakes up three days before a new hire's start date and checks that provisioning is on track. A compliance agent that reviews access permissions weekly and flags anomalies.

This is the shift from agents you call to agents that show up on their own — taking initiative, not just instructions.

🔗 The network of work agents

Individual agents are powerful. But the real shift is when they work together.

Consider this chain: A change management agent deploys a code update. An observability agent is monitoring the environment — it detects increased error rates in the affected service. It flags the issue and the incident agent kicks in: pulls the relevant runbook, checks the on-call schedule through PagerDuty, pages the right engineer, and posts a structured incident summary in Slack with the likely root cause, what's been affected, and the recommended response. Once the incident is resolved, it loops back to the change agent to tag the deployment as the root cause for future reference.

No human orchestrated that chain. Each agent did its job and passed the baton to the next one when its part was done. This is what we mean by the AI Workforce — not a single AI doing everything, but a network of specialists coordinating across functions, the same way a well-run operations team would.

In fact, this pattern already exists in a simpler form today: when Atom receives a request and hands off to a specialized agent, that's multi-agent collaboration. What we're building toward is this happening across more agents, across more systems, with less human orchestration in between.

📋

Agent lifecycle management

Creating, versioning, updating, and retiring agents with proper change control. Just like onboarding and offboarding a team member.

👁

Observability

Full visibility into what each agent did, why it made the decisions it made, and what the outcomes were. Not just logs — meaningful audit trails.

🔐

Identity and access management for agents

Agents operate with defined permissions, the same way human employees do. What systems can they access? What actions can they take? What requires escalation?

🤝

Agent-to-agent coordination

Defined patterns for how agents request help from each other, handle conflicting instructions, and resolve handoffs cleanly.

📊

Cost and performance tracking

Understanding what each agent costs to operate and whether it's delivering value. The economics of your AI workforce, visible and manageable.

"What's next for Atom?" ▶

Atom is becoming the front door to a team of AI specialists. Today it's a generalist assistant. We're building toward a model where behind Atom are specialized agents — each with a defined role, specific skills, and access to the tools they need. You'll be able to create agents that handle your organization's specific processes.

"How is this different from Copilot / Agentforce / other agent platforms?" ▶

Two things. First, our agents live inside a service management platform — they natively understand your catalog, your assets, your people, your tickets, and they have real-time context about your users and devices. "Is this replacing Atom?" ▶

No. Atom is the generalist — the front door employees talk to. Specialized agents work behind Atom, handling specific domains. When an employee's request falls within a specialist's area, Atom hands off seamlessly. The employee experience doesn't change.

Build Exercise

This is the best test of whether you've internalized the framework. If you can write a plausible agent.md, you understand how agents work in Atomicwork.

The brief

Write an agent.md for a Legal Contract Review Agent at a mid-size company. The company uses DocuSign for contract management, Slack for communication, and has a legal knowledge base in Atomicwork with standard contract templates and approved clause libraries.

The agent should help employees across the company who need contracts reviewed — NDAs, vendor agreements, SOWs. It should be able to handle standard reviews autonomously and escalate non-standard requests to the legal team.

Define:

  • Identity (name, role, audience, purpose, tone)
  • Behavior (guardrails, organizational context, escalation rules)
  • At least two skills
  • Tools

There's no single right answer — but your agent should reflect the principles from this guide: autonomous where appropriate, keeps humans in the loop for judgment calls, uses platform context, and has clear guardrails.

Get Certified

20 questions, ~15 minutes. Mix of single-select, multi-select, and classification questions. These are designed to test understanding, not recall — you'll need to think about how the concepts apply. Score 80% or higher to pass.
